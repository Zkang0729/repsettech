We Interviewed A New Gym Goer In The CRC.

Here's What We Got...

What was the hardest part about starting to go to the gym?

The hardest part was the first few workouts I had after going to the gym for the first time. My muscles were sore and I needed to increase the weights every workout. I couldn’t walk or move properly for the first week.

How did you ease into working out?

Having a set schedule and a friend to work out with was what made it easy. Without a set schedule, I feel that I would either procrastinate or not feel motivated to go. Making workout a part of my daily schedule helped a lot. Furthermore, having a friend to go with me was not only made the workout fun in general but he also kept me in check.

What is one piece of advice you would give to a beginner in the gym?

Have a set schedule and make sure to follow it. Don’t overwork yourself because the faster you move up the weight, the harder it becomes and the less motivating it becomes. There is no reason to rush to take it slow. Also, finding a friend or gym partner to go with you might ensure the longevity of the workout.

How did your nutrition change?

I tried not to make drastic changes to my diet. Suddenly changing the amount you eat or changing the type of food you eat might affect your weight in the short run but your body will ultimately return to its original state due to the yo-yo effect. I only cut out what was bad for me which included any drinks other than water and milk and decreased the amount of fried food. I also try to not eat anything after 10 P.M.

What are your goals?

My goal with my body is to become leaner and become stronger over a long period of time. This would mean controlling my diet and working out with routine. My ultimate goal is to get into a habit of exercising and working out so that it carries over to when I am older.

What are three things anyone who is beginning to workout should know?

This will be very case by case, but in general anyone new to a gym setting should know they need to be patient, have realistic goals, and to keep safety in mind at all times.

What is the hardest part for most beginners in the gym?

It depends what makes someone start going to the gym, but motivation and intimidation in the gym setting are probably two of the biggest factors many people have to overcome when starting to workout.

How do you stay dedicated/motivated?

This will vary person to person too. Some people find putting gym/workout time on their calendar helps with accountability. If possible, having a buddy meeting you to workout also helps.

What is the best nutritional tip you can give? Best supplements for a beginner?

This is out of the scope of practice of personal trainers. Trainers can only give very general advice, nothing personalized, such as information available from myplate.gov.

How to protect themselves? How to keep oneself from getting injured?

If possible, working out with someone who is familiar with exercise is a great option. Having someone who can help keep an eye on your form is very helpful. If you workout alone, keeping your ego in line and don’t worry about how much weight you are doing, but rather than anything you do you are doing correctly will help prevent injuries. Not taking on too much too fast will help as well. Always listen to your body if rest or recovery are needed.

Tips For Different Workout Goals

Getting Stronger

If you want to strictly get stronger, the assumption is that you are lifting weights or something along the lines of that. It is harder to get stronger without gaining weight/muscle because, at a certain point, your body will reach its peak strength levels. In order to become stronger, your muscles must tear and rebuild larger than before. This means you will gain weight. Assuming you have a rigorous enough workout routine (at least 3 days a week with heavier weights), the first nutritional step you should take is to go to the calorie calculator mentioned earlier to find your maintenance caloric intake. In order to get stronger, you will need to build muscle, so it’s recommended that you try to add about 500 calories to your caloric intake. This usually will guarantee a gain in weight and muscle mass. A good way to keep track of all of this is the app MyFitnessPal. It shows you what macronutrients you need for each day and you can input the foods you eat to make sure you hit those goals. It can also track your weight so you can see if you’re gaining weight or not. If things aren’t going smoothly, you can adjust the settings or your goals.

Gain Weight

If you are looking to strictly gain weight, the only thing you need to do is eat 500 more calories per day than your maintenance caloric intake and use the app mentioned to keep track of your progress.

Build Muscle

If you want to mainly build muscle, there isn’t much room to be super unhealthy with your diet. IIFYM won’t yield the best results because there are many processed foods and simple sugars that could lead to fat gain (fruits, sugar, white bread, soda, syrup, baked treats, and kid’s cereal). It is impossible for a regular person with a regular body to gain muscle without gaining any fat unless maybe if you already had the muscle before and you’re regaining it, but this website is for beginners. If you are a beginner on the more overweight side of the spectrum, it is possible to lose fat and gain muscle at the same time. But as a regular person, if you want to gain the least amount of fat possible while still building muscle, you need to stay extremely disciplined in your workout routine and diet. If you’re gaining weight too fast, decrease the amount of added calories to your maintenance caloric intake.

Lose Weight

If you want to strictly lose weight, eat about 500 fewer calories per day than your maintenance caloric intake. If this is too much of a difference, you can ease into it by starting out with -100 calories. Exercise isn’t necessarily required to lose weight, but it’s easier if you do exercise. Exercise will burn calories, which means it’s easier for you to eat fewer net calories in a day.

Lose Fat

Losing fat requires a certain amount of discipline because it means that you want to keep most, if not all of the muscle, you have now and only cut the fat. As mentioned in the above bullet, you have to eat fewer calories, but in order to make sure your muscles don’t disappear, you should increase your protein intake. This protein intake is referring to the amount recommended by the app mentioned earlier or, to make it simple, 1 gram of protein per lb of body weight (1.5 grams if you are skinnier). So for example, you could go from 220 grams to 250 grams of protein a day.

There are some specific diets that are popular for losing fat.

  • Intermittent Fasting - “an eating pattern that cycles between periods of fasting and eating”. You can basically eat anything you want, but it has to be within a certain window. If you want the best results, it’s best to eat healthier foods. The most common method is the 16/8 method which is when you restrict your eating to only 8 hours of the day. This is usually from noon to about 8:00 pm, but it all depends on your preference.
  • The Ketogenic Diet - “The ketogenic diet is a very low-carb, high-fat diet that shares many similarities with the Atkins and low-carb diets. It involves drastically reducing carbohydrate intake and replacing it with fat. This reduction in carbs puts your body into a metabolic state called ketosis. When this happens, your body becomes incredibly efficient at burning fat for energy. It also turns fat into ketones in the liver, which can supply energy for the brain.”

Stay Healthy

Be Healthy This is pretty broad because it all depends on what your perception of healthy is. This section uses a common consensus when it comes to being healthy. Healthy is being within your recommended weight range, exercising on a consistent basis, and eating more organic/“real” foods and less processed foods.
